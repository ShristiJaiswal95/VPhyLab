// 01 — Aim
Objective of the Experiment
What this virtual lab demonstrates

Primary Aim

To visualize and study the Fermi-Dirac probability distribution f(E) of electrons in a semiconductor as a function of temperature and Fermi level position.

Secondary Aim

To observe how thermal excitation causes electrons to jump from the valence band to the conduction band, generating free carriers and increasing conductivity.

Simulation Goal

To animate electron behaviour at the atomic scale and connect it to macroscopic properties like resistance, band gap, and carrier concentration.

Syllabus Connection

Directly reinforces Unit 1 topics: energy bands, Fermi level, probability of occupation, density of states, carrier concentration vs temperature.

// 02 — Theory
Physics Background
The quantum mechanics behind electron distribution

Energy Bands (Kronig-Penney Model)

Electrons in a crystal cannot have continuous energies. They are confined to allowed bands separated by forbidden band gaps. The valence band is filled at 0 K; the conduction band is empty.

Fermi Level EF

The Fermi level is the energy at which the probability of occupation is exactly 0.5. For an intrinsic semiconductor, EF lies near the middle of the band gap. Doping shifts it up (n-type) or down (p-type).

Temperature Effect

As temperature T rises, thermal energy kT increases. More electrons gain enough energy to jump across the gap Eg, creating electron-hole pairs. Conductivity rises exponentially with T.

Direct vs Indirect Gap

In a direct gap (GaAs), electrons transition without phonon assistance — ideal for LEDs/lasers. In an indirect gap (Si, Ge), phonons are needed for momentum conservation, making optical emission inefficient.

// 03 — Procedure
Experimental Steps
How to perform the POB experiment and use this simulation
01
Set Up the Circuit
Connect the semiconductor sample in the Wheatstone bridge using a Post Office Box. Connect DC supply and galvanometer. Immerse the semiconductor in an oil bath with a thermometer.
02
Heat the Sample
Heat the oil bath slowly from room temperature (~300 K) to ~370 K. Allow the system to reach thermal equilibrium at each temperature before recording.
03
Record R and T
Balance the bridge at each temperature by adjusting POB. Record T in Kelvin and corresponding resistance R in Ω every 5°C rise.
04
Calculate ln(R) and 1/T
For each reading, compute 1/T and ln(R). Enter values in the observation table.
05
Plot the Graph
Plot ln(R) vs 1/T. The result is a straight line. Calculate slope = Δln(R) / Δ(1/T).
06
Extract Band Gap
Calculate: Eg = 2 × kB × slope. Compare with reference values. The simulation below verifies your result computationally.
